Biodiversity crisis:
No sale, no kill

Species population

In the last decade, approximately 9,396 African rhinos have been killed due to poaching. From 2008, poaching numbers continued to increase across Africa until peaking in 2015, when 1,349 rhinos were killed. Fortunately, poaching has declined across the continent since then, but at least one rhino is still killed every day. The problem is particularly acute in South Africa, where more than 1,000 rhinos were killed each year between 2013 and 2017. 448 rhinos were killed in South Africa in 2022, a decrease of three from 2021(Save the Rhino, 2018).

Regarding elephants, there are about 450,000 of them worldwide. African elephants are approximately ten times more numerous than Asian elephants. in 2015, African elephants numbered approximately 415,000, while Asian elephants numbered between 40,000 and 50,000. However, the African elephant population has shrunk by 98% over the past few centuries, from 25 million in 1500. It is estimated that for every 100 elephants alive, about 12 have died. This indicates that elephant populations are declining. And Asian elephant populations have halved in the past century, from 100,000 in the early 1900s to 40,000 to 50,000 today(Ritchie, 2022c).

Roles of animals in nature

Whale

Whales are critical to the food chain, stabilizing food flow and maintaining healthy oceans. As a result, a rapid decline in whale populations would have a major impact on marine ecosystems. Whales are critical to maintaining healthy marine life by regulating ocean food flow to maintain a healthy balance between different species. Whales play an important role in the energy flow and biological pump of marine life as a whole(Lian & Lu, 2019).

Elephant

Elephants, as the largest land animals, are crucial for biodiversity. They create paths in dense vegetation, offering food and shelter to other animals. This behavior aids in preventing wildfires by controlling shrub growth. Moreover, elephants facilitate plant diversity by dispersing seeds. Their movements leave tracks that collect rainwater, serving as a vital water source for animals during the dry season.(David Shepherd Wildlife Foundation, 2020).

Rhino

Rhinos are crucial to the ecosystem as long-standing species. They consume vegetation, shaping the African landscape and maintaining ecosystem balance. Local communities rely on rhino habitats for food, fuel, and income. As part of the "Big Five," rhinos attract tourists, providing income through ecotourism. Protecting rhinos safeguards their habitat, benefiting both people and wildlife. This work supports local communities and ensures future generations can enjoy natural resources.(WWF, 2014).

Hunting purposes

Whale

People historically hunted whales primarily for whale oil, which was widely used for lighting (e.g., street lamps, household lamps), lubricants, and even skin care products. Its demand peaked in the late 19th century but declined and eventually ceased with the introduction of oil and other alternatives. Additionally, whale meat serves as a food source in countries like Japan, Norway, and the United States.(Ritchie, 2022a)。

Elephant

Elephants are hunted for their valuable ivory, which is in high demand, particularly in parts of Asia where it is considered a luxury item. There is also a market for elephant meat and skins. Despite efforts to curb the illegal ivory trade, elephants remain at risk of hunting due to the high value of these products.

African elephants face large-scale hunting for ivory and habitat loss due to deforestation and agricultural expansion. Asian elephants are also affected but have a lower risk of poaching due to their less abundant tusks.(Ritchie, 2022b.).

Rhino

Rhino hunting primarily occurs due to the demand for their horn and body parts. Rhino horn is highly valued as a luxury item and is used in traditional Chinese medicine and other purposes across Asia. Despite a ban on its medical use since 1993, the persistent belief in its medicinal properties fuels the demand for rhino horn, leading to poaching of these majestic animals. (Ritchie, 2022b; IAPF, 2021).

In addition to the fact that these animals are experiencing such encounters, there are local examples of the following animals that have been hunted in Australia:

Sea Turtle：

Sea turtles are hunted for their distinctive amber, yellow and brown shells, which are made into souvenirs and accessories, such as jewelry, hair combs and sunglasses(WWF, 2018).

Kangaroo：

Australia's kangaroo industry relies on the hunting of large kangaroos to produce meat and leather products(Assoune, 2020；Animal Australia, n.d.-b).

Other native animals:

Some of Australia's iconic animals, such as dolphins, flying foxes and wombats, are also legally hunted under federal Aboriginal land laws, even though they are considered endangered((Animal Australia, n.d.-a)).

What we can do

Rhino populations have significantly increased due to global conservation efforts. Protection measures, especially in African nature reserves, have led to a remarkable rise in rhino numbers in the 20th century. The World Wildlife Fund (WWF) has played a vital role in rhino conservation by protecting and relocating rhinos, as well as supporting community-based conservation. They also combat poaching through innovative techniques, bolstering patrolling capacity, and advocating for stronger enforcement against illegal trade.(Ritchie, 2022b；(WWF, 2022)).

Current rhino population status: There are five species of rhinoceros, three of which (Sumatran rhinoceros, Javan rhinoceros and black rhinoceros) are considered "critically endangered". The other two species, the white rhinoceros and the Indian rhinoceros (greater single-horned rhinoceros), have shown some degree of recovery over the past few decades.

At the beginning of the 20th century, the southern white rhino population plummeted to just 20 individuals. However, after conservation efforts in the 20th century, the population has recovered to over 21,000. However, increased poaching in recent years has caused the population to decline again.

The Indian rhino population has grown from about 40 in the middle of the last century to more than 4,000 today, thanks to conservation and poaching reduction efforts in India and Nepal.

Call for animal protection

The conservation of animals such as rhinos, elephants and whales is a top priority. They play an important role in the ecosystem, but populations are declining dramatically. To save them, we need to take action. Increased enforcement against poaching, public awareness, habitat protection, international cooperation and global awareness are essential. Protecting animals is not only about them, but also about the survival of the Earth's ecosystem and biodiversity. No sale, no harm, now is the time to act, let's work together to protect these precious creatures and create a better world for the future.
